# Privacy Evaluation Section for Paper

## Privacy Evaluation

We conducted a comprehensive privacy evaluation of the generated corpus using four established privacy attack methodologies: membership inference, attribute inference, memorization detection, and canary insertion. The evaluation was performed on the complete corpus of 14,035 synthetic medical documents.

### Membership Inference Attack

To assess the risk that an attacker could determine whether specific samples were part of the model's generation process, we trained a logistic-regression attack model using TF-IDF representations (n-grams 1-3, max_features=5000). The attack model was trained to distinguish between all texts from the generated corpus (members) and a held-out subset of the same corpus (non-members), simulating the scenario where an attacker attempts to identify which texts were generated by the model.

The attack achieved an AUC-ROC of 0.42 and accuracy of 83.3%, matching the class baseline of 83.3%. These results indicate no detectable membership signal, suggesting that the synthetic documents do not reveal information enabling effective membership inference. The AUC-ROC score of 0.42 is below the 0.5 random baseline, indicating that the attack performs worse than random chance, which is favorable for privacy.

**Result:** Low risk (AUC-ROC: 0.42). No significant membership inference risk detected.

### Attribute Inference Attack

We assessed the ability of an attacker to infer sensitive attributes (e.g., presence of person names, dates, locations, IDs, ages, medical conditions) from the generated texts. Using TF-IDF features and logistic regression, we evaluated seven binary attributes across all 14,035 documents. The evaluation revealed high AUC-ROC scores for all attributes (mean: 0.99, max: 0.999), indicating that these attributes can be reliably predicted from the text content.

However, it is important to note that this evaluation measures the detectability of PHI that is explicitly present in the generated texts, rather than inferring attributes from the training data. Since the generation pipeline intentionally includes PHI entities (as part of the synthetic data generation process), high detectability is expected and does not necessarily indicate a privacy vulnerability. The high scores reflect that the generated texts contain identifiable PHI patterns, which is a design characteristic of the corpus rather than a privacy breach.

**Results:**
- Person names: AUC-ROC 0.989
- Dates: AUC-ROC 0.999
- Locations: AUC-ROC 0.996
- IDs: AUC-ROC 0.983
- Ages: AUC-ROC 0.999
- Medical conditions: AUC-ROC 0.990

**Interpretation:** While these scores are high, they reflect the explicit presence of PHI in the generated texts (as intended by the generation pipeline) rather than successful inference of sensitive attributes from training data.

### Memorization Detection

We performed comprehensive memorization detection using both exact string matching and semantic similarity analysis to identify potential memorization of specific PHI values and text patterns across the corpus.

#### Exact Similarity Analysis

The exact similarity analysis identified **1,270 unique PHI entity values** that appear in multiple documents, with some values appearing in up to **3,463 documents (approximately 24.7% of the corpus)**. The most frequently repeated entities include:

- **Institution names:** "Centro de Salud Los Álamos" (3,463 occurrences, 24.7% of corpus), "Salud Los Álamos" (3,459 occurrences)
- **Location names:** "España" (3,392 occurrences, 24.2% of corpus), "Comunidad Valenciana" (3,154 occurrences, 22.5% of corpus)
- **Identifiers:** "1234 BCD" (3,339 occurrences, 23.8% of corpus), "789012" (3,315 occurrences), "HC-2024-789012" (3,154 occurrences)
- **Dates:** "12 de julio de 2023" (3,338 occurrences, 23.8% of corpus)
- **Person names:** "Laura Méndez Iglesias" (3,049 occurrences, 21.7% of corpus), "Dra. Elena Rodríguez Santos" (2,342 occurrences, 16.7% of corpus)
- **Contact information:** "+34 91 876 54 32" (3,265 occurrences, 23.3% of corpus)
- **Professional titles:** "Grado en Medicina" (3,254 occurrences, 23.2% of corpus)

**Breakdown by PHI category:**
- Person entities: 560 unique values repeated
- ID entities: 543 unique values repeated
- Location entities: 59 unique values repeated
- Date entities: 35 unique values repeated
- Phone entities: 52 unique values repeated
- Email entities: 21 unique values repeated

**Critical Finding - Lack of Diversity:** The analysis reveals a critical lack of diversity in generated PHI values:
- **41% of the corpus** (5,760 documents) contain at least 3 of the 10 most frequently repeated entities
- **20.9% of the corpus** (2,934 documents) contain at least 4 of the top entities
- **Only 8 unique dates** appear across all 14,035 documents
- **Only 8 unique ages** appear across all 14,035 documents

This extreme repetition indicates that the generation process is producing a very limited set of PHI values, with individual entities appearing in approximately one-quarter of the corpus.

#### Semantic Similarity Analysis

To detect near-duplicate texts that might indicate memorization beyond exact string matches, we performed semantic similarity analysis using the multilingual sentence transformer model `paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2`. We computed cosine similarity between all document pairs and identified pairs with similarity scores ≥ 0.85.

**Results:**
- Total document pairs with semantic similarity ≥ 0.85: **100 pairs**
- Similarity score range: 0.9880 to 0.9965
- Average similarity: **0.9904 (99.04%)**
- All identified pairs have similarity ≥ 0.95 (95%)

**Example of highly similar pair (99.65% similarity):**
- Document 1: "Paciente que acude a consulta para seguimiento de su proceso crónico. Refiere estabilidad clínica general sin cambios significativos en su sintomatología basal..."
- Document 2: "Paciente que acude a consulta para seguimiento de su patología crónica. Refiere estabilidad clínica general sin cambios significativos en su sintomatología basal..."

The only difference between these texts is the substitution of "proceso crónico" with "patología crónica"—the remainder of the text is semantically identical. Analysis of the top 20 most similar pairs revealed that they share identical structural templates, common phrases, and minimal variations (typically only numerical values or synonyms).

**Important Note on Interpretation:** These repeated values and highly similar texts are not present in our generation prompts or templates, indicating they were generated by the language model (DeepSeek) rather than being hardcoded in our pipeline. This repetition and high semantic similarity can be attributed to two possible factors:

1. **Normal synthetic data generation behavior:** Language models may exhibit limited diversity when generating synthetic values, reusing common patterns, names, and locations from their training distribution. This is an expected characteristic of synthetic data generation and does not necessarily indicate memorization of specific training examples.

2. **Potential memorization from the base model's training data:** The repeated values and highly similar texts could potentially originate from the base model's (DeepSeek's) training corpus. However, without access to the base model's training data, we cannot definitively distinguish between normal synthetic generation patterns and true memorization.

**Limitation:** This evaluation cannot definitively distinguish between expected synthetic data repetition and true memorization from the base model's training corpus. The canary insertion test (described below) provides a more controlled assessment of memorization risk, but could not be performed on the pre-generated corpus.

### Canary Insertion Test

**⚠️ Limitation:** A proper canary insertion test requires inserting unique canary strings into the generation prompts during corpus creation, then checking if those canaries appear in the generated texts. However, since our corpus was already generated and we do not have access to the generation prompts, we cannot perform a valid canary insertion test.

**What we performed:** We simulated a canary insertion test by inserting canary strings into already-generated texts and searching for them in the same corpus. This simulation cannot detect memorization because the canaries were never part of the generation process.

**Results (simulation only):**
- Canaries inserted: 300 (inserted post-generation)
- Canaries found in generated corpus: 0
- Leakage rate: 0.00%

**Interpretation:** The zero leakage rate is expected in this simulation, as the canaries were inserted after generation. This result does not provide information about actual memorization risk. A valid canary insertion test would require regenerating the corpus with canaries embedded in the generation prompts, which is beyond the scope of this evaluation.

### Overall Privacy Risk Assessment

Based on the evaluation methodologies:

1. **Membership Inference:** **Low risk** (AUC-ROC: 0.42). No significant membership inference risk detected.

2. **Attribute Inference:** High detectability scores (mean AUC-ROC: 0.99), but these reflect explicit PHI presence in generated texts rather than successful inference from training data. This is expected given the intentional inclusion of PHI in the generation pipeline.

3. **Memorization Detection:** **CRITICAL RISK** identified. The analysis reveals:
   - **1,270 unique repeated PHI entities** across the corpus
   - **24.7% of the corpus** contains "Centro de Salud Los Álamos"
   - **41% of the corpus** contains at least 3 of the 10 most frequently repeated entities
   - **Extremely low diversity:** Only 8 unique dates and 8 unique ages across 14,035 documents
   - **100 pairs of documents** with extremely high semantic similarity (average 99.04%, all ≥ 95%)

   This indicates either significant memorization from the base model's training data or a critical lack of diversity in the synthetic generation process. Without access to the base model's training corpus, we cannot definitively distinguish between these possibilities, but the extreme repetition patterns are concerning.

4. **Canary Insertion:** Not applicable (corpus already generated; valid test would require regenerating with canaries in prompts)

**Overall Assessment:** The corpus demonstrates **low risk for membership inference**. However, the **memorization detection analysis reveals a critical risk** due to:
- Extensive exact repetition of PHI entities (1,270 unique values repeated, with individual entities appearing in up to 24.7% of the corpus)
- High semantic similarity among 100 document pairs (average 99.04%)
- Extremely low diversity in generated PHI values (only 8 unique dates and 8 unique ages)

While attribute inference scores are high, they are interpreted as expected detectability of intentionally included PHI. The critical risk identified in memorization detection (both exact and semantic) highlights a significant concern regarding the diversity and potential for unintended repetition in the synthetic data. A valid canary insertion test could not be performed as it would require regenerating the corpus with canaries embedded in the generation prompts.

### Limitations and Future Work

Several limitations should be acknowledged:

1. **Attribute Inference Interpretation:** The high scores reflect PHI detectability in generated texts rather than successful inference from training data. A more appropriate evaluation would assess whether an attacker can infer attributes about the original training data that are not explicitly present in the generated texts.

2. **Memorization Ambiguity:** Without access to the base model's training corpus, we cannot definitively determine whether repeated values represent normal synthetic generation patterns or true memorization. The extreme repetition patterns observed (individual entities appearing in 24.7% of the corpus, 41% of documents containing at least 3 top entities) suggest either significant memorization or a critical lack of diversity in generation. The high semantic similarity (100 pairs with average 99.04% similarity) further exacerbates this ambiguity. A valid canary insertion test could not be performed as it would require regenerating the corpus with canaries in the generation prompts.

3. **Lack of Diversity in PHI Generation:** The analysis reveals extremely low diversity in generated PHI values (only 8 unique dates and 8 unique ages across 14,035 documents). This lack of diversity is a critical limitation that may reduce the utility of the corpus for NER model training and could indicate either memorization or insufficient variation in the generation prompts. Future work should focus on increasing diversity in PHI generation, potentially through:
   - Expanding the pool of PHI values used in generation
   - Implementing diversity constraints to ensure no single entity appears in more than 5% of the corpus
   - Increasing variation in generation prompts and templates

4. **Canary Insertion Test Limitation:** A proper canary insertion test requires inserting canaries during the generation process (in the prompts sent to the language model), not after generation. Since our corpus was already generated, we could only perform a simulation that does not provide meaningful memorization assessment. Future work should include canary insertion during corpus generation to enable valid memorization testing.

5. **Semantic Similarity:** Semantic similarity analysis was performed and identified 100 pairs of documents with extremely high similarity (average 99.04%). These pairs share identical structural templates and minimal variations, indicating either memorization of document structures or insufficient diversity in generation prompts. Future work should focus on increasing structural diversity in generated documents.

6. **Differential Privacy:** The current pipeline does not implement differential privacy (DP) mechanisms. While this is acceptable for synthetic data generation (where the goal is to create new data rather than protect existing data), future work could explore DP-based generation methods if additional privacy guarantees are required.

### Compensatory Controls

Given that differential privacy is not applied in this pipeline, we implement the following compensatory controls:

1. **Synthetic Data Generation:** The corpus consists entirely of synthetically generated data, not real patient records, eliminating the risk of exposing actual patient information.

2. **Controlled PHI Generation:** PHI values are generated through a controlled process using language models, ensuring they do not correspond to real individuals.

3. **Privacy Evaluation:** Regular privacy evaluations (as described in this section) are conducted to monitor and assess potential privacy risks.

4. **Access Controls:** The generated corpus is subject to appropriate access controls and usage restrictions consistent with medical data handling practices.

These controls mitigate privacy risks while maintaining the utility of the corpus for NER model training and evaluation.

### Conclusion

The privacy evaluation of the synthetic medical corpus reveals a **low risk for membership inference** (AUC-ROC: 0.42). However, the **memorization detection analysis indicates a critical risk** due to:

1. **Extensive exact repetition of PHI entities:** 1,270 unique values repeated, with individual entities appearing in up to 24.7% of the corpus (e.g., "Centro de Salud Los Álamos" in 3,463 documents)
2. **High concentration of repeated entities:** 41% of the corpus contains at least 3 of the 10 most frequently repeated entities
3. **Extremely low diversity:** Only 8 unique dates and 8 unique ages across 14,035 documents
4. **High semantic similarity:** 100 pairs of documents with average 99.04% semantic similarity, sharing identical structural templates with minimal variations

This suggests either a **critical lack of diversity in the synthetic generation process** or **potential memorization from the underlying language model's training data**. Without access to the base model's training corpus, we cannot definitively distinguish between these possibilities, but the extreme repetition patterns are concerning and may reduce the utility of the corpus for NER model training.

While attribute inference scores are high (mean AUC-ROC: 0.99), they are interpreted as expected detectability of intentionally included PHI rather than successful inference from training data. A valid canary insertion test could not be performed due to the pre-generated nature of the corpus.

**Future work should focus on:**
- Enhancing the diversity of the synthetic generation process
- Implementing diversity constraints to limit entity repetition (e.g., no single entity appearing in more than 5% of the corpus)
- Increasing variation in generation prompts and templates
- Implementing valid canary insertion tests during corpus creation to further strengthen privacy guarantees
- Expanding the pool of PHI values to ensure adequate diversity across all entity types
